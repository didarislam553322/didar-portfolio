Common Drop-Off Points in User Onboarding Funnels

Technical 3 min read · Didar Islam

An onboarding funnel is the path a user takes from the first time they see your product to the moment they get real value from it. Each step in that path loses some people. This post breaks down the five places most products lose users, and why the early steps matter most.

Why Small Losses Add Up Fast

Funnel steps multiply against each other. Say a product has five steps, with these completion rates:

  • Landing page to sign-up: 30%
  • Sign-up to account setup: 60%
  • Setup to first action taken: 50%
  • First action to real value delivered: 55%
  • Real value to a repeat visit: 40%

Multiply those together (0.30 × 0.60 × 0.50 × 0.55 × 0.40) and only 1.98% of visitors make it all the way through. Out of 10,000 visitors, that is 198 people who become real, regular users.

This is why fixing even one step pays off across the whole funnel. Raise setup completion from 50% to 65%, and the total jumps to 2.57%, which is 30% more users, from one fix.

The Five Places Users Drop Off

  1. They do not get it (landing page to sign-up). Visitors leave because they cannot tell what the product does. A simple test: show someone the page for five seconds and ask them to explain what it offers. This step matters most for DTC (direct-to-consumer) products, meaning products sold straight to the customer with no salesperson in between to explain things later.

  2. The sign-up form is annoying (sign-up to setup). Every extra field you ask for loses people. The fix is to only ask what you need for the very first session, and ask for the rest later, once the person has already seen some value.

  3. The account is empty (setup to first action). A brand-new account with nothing in it gives the user nothing to do. The fix is to add templates, sample data, or an import option, so the user starts with something to work with instead of a blank screen.

  4. It takes too long to matter (first action to real value). This is the time between when a user starts using the product and when they actually get the benefit they came for. Teams should define that "value moment" clearly and build the shortest possible path to it. This is what's meant by customer-centric design: building around what the user wants, not around a tour of your features.

  5. They never come back (value to return visit). A user who got value once but never returned still left. The fix is timed follow-ups (emails, in-app messages) sent through a CRM (Customer Relationship Management tool, like HubSpot) based on what the user actually did, not just a fixed schedule.

Finding the Real Cause

The numbers show you where people are leaving. To find out why, watch session recordings, run tests on prototypes (tools like Figma work well), or map the user's journey with a team using something like FigJam. A post-signup survey using CSAT (Customer Satisfaction Score, the percent of users who rate their experience positively) adds a second data point alongside what people actually did.

These five steps usually get split across teams: marketing owns step one, product owns steps two through four, and customer success (the team responsible for making sure users actually succeed with the product) owns step five. That split is often the real problem. Each team optimizes its own piece while the losses multiply between them. The simplest fix is one person who owns the full funnel and reviews it every month.

Bottom Line

Track all five steps, multiply them out, and fix the step with the steepest drop and the cheapest fix first. Onboarding fixes pay off again and again, since every new user goes through the same funnel.
