Outcome-Based Roadmapping vs. Feature-List Roadmapping

Strategy 3 min read · Didar Islam

A product roadmap is a plan that shows what a team will build and when. There are two common ways to build one. Each has real trade-offs.

The Feature-List Roadmap

This type of roadmap lists specific features tied to dates. For example, a software team's roadmap might say:

  • Q1: bulk import, meaning a tool that lets a user upload many records at once (like a spreadsheet of 500 contacts) instead of typing them in one by one.
  • Q2: mobile app, meaning a version of the product that works on a phone or tablet, not just a desktop browser.
  • Q3: reporting module, meaning a built-in dashboard that turns raw data into charts and summaries, so users do not have to export numbers and build their own spreadsheet.

Each of these becomes a task with a start date, an end date, and an owner, which is why this format works well with scheduling tools like Asana or Smartsheet.

The upside: it is easy to read and easy to track. Sales teams can promise a feature by a certain date. Executives can measure progress as a percentage complete.

The downside: the team locks in a solution before they know if it actually works. If bulk import ships in Q1 and no one uses it, the roadmap still shows it as done. Shipping got measured. Results did not.

The Outcome-Based Roadmap

This roadmap is built around a measurable change in behavior, not a feature. For example: "Q1: cut the time it takes a new account to get value from 9 days to 3." In PM (product management) terms, this measurable change is called an outcome.

Features become hypotheses, meaning educated guesses that get tested against that outcome. If a feature does not move the number, the team drops it and tries something else. The roadmap itself does not need to change.

A quick example: say a team has three feature ideas that might improve sign-ups, with expected gains of 8%, 5%, and 3%. They only have time to build two. A feature-list roadmap forces them to pick all their bets now, before they have any real data. An outcome-based roadmap ships the 8% idea first and measures it. If the real lift turns out to be 2%, the team learns that at the cost of one feature, not three.

It Depends on the Product's Stage

Early-stage products benefit more from outcome-based roadmaps, since there is more uncertainty and a wrong guess costs more. Mature products with paying enterprise customers often need feature-list roadmaps, since big clients want a firm date before they sign a contract.

GTM (go-to-market) strategy, meaning the plan for launching and selling a product, also plays a role. A launch tied to marketing and sales needs a fixed date to work.

The Practical Fix

Most teams do best with a mix: firm dates for the next quarter, and outcome goals for the quarters after that.

Write every roadmap item as a number to hit. List features underneath as your best current guesses for hitting it. Save firm dates for the few things that truly need them, like a client contract or a public launch.

That keeps the team focused on the only question that matters: is the product measurably better than it was last quarter?
