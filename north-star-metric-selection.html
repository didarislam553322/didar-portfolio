Selecting and Validating a North Star Metric

Frameworks 3 min read · Didar Islam

A north star metric is the one number a team treats as its main sign of success. It exists to solve a simple problem: when every team tracks its own numbers, no one is actually pulling in the same direction. This post covers how to pick one, how to test it with a real example, and how to check that it actually works.

Three Rules for Picking One

A good north star metric needs to pass three tests.

  1. It measures value the user actually got, not value you took from them. Revenue is a result of a good metric, not the metric itself. Revenue can go up for a while even as users get less value, for example if you raise prices on customers who feel stuck with you.

  2. It moves before the business results do. This is called a leading indicator, meaning a number that changes early and warns you what is coming, before things like churn (customers leaving) or revenue show up in the numbers.

  3. Every team can actually move it. If only the sales team can affect the number, it cannot guide the whole company.

A Real Example

Take a spreadsheet collaboration tool. Three possible metrics:

  • Registered accounts. Fails rule one. Someone who signs up and never comes back got zero value.
  • Weekly active users. Better, but still fuzzy. Someone who opens the app for five seconds and leaves still counts as "active."
  • Documents edited by two or more people per week. This passes all three tests. It captures the actual point of the product (working together), it predicts future revenue (teams that collaborate tend to stick around and pay), and every team, from engineering to marketing, can influence it.

You can break that metric down into the pieces that build it:

Multi-editor documents per week = new accounts × activation rate × collaboration rate × weekly retention

(Activation rate means the percent of new users who actually start using the product. Retention means the percent who are still around later.)

Say the current numbers are 1,000 new accounts, 40% activation, 30% collaboration, and 60% retention. Multiply those out and you get 72 multi-editor documents a week from each new group of sign-ups. This breakdown shows exactly where to focus. Raise activation from 40% to 50%, and that number jumps to 90, a 25% gain from changing just one piece.

Measuring It and Checking It's Real

To track this, you need event-level data, meaning data on individual actions users take, not just totals. Tools like Amplitude or Mixpanel track behavior (activation, collaboration, retention), while tools like Google Analytics or Adobe Analytics usually track how people found you in the first place. Dashboard tools like Looker or Tableau then pull it all into one view everyone can read the same way.

Here is the step most teams skip: testing whether the metric actually predicts anything. Take past customers, group them by how much they did the "north star" behavior in their first month, and compare how many were still around a year later.

If customers who collaborated a lot early on kept 85% retention, while customers who barely collaborated kept only 40%, the metric is proven. If both groups look the same a year later, the metric does not actually predict anything, and you should pick a different one.

Bottom Line

Pick a metric that reflects real user value, shows up early, and every team can move. Break it into its parts so people know exactly what to work on. Then test it against real retention numbers before asking the whole company to chase it. A metric that fails that test is worse than having no metric, since it points everyone at a number that does not matter.
