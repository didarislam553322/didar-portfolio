Structuring a Product Requirements Document: Section-by-Section Rationale

Frameworks 3 min read · Didar Islam

A Product Requirements Document (PRD) is the artifact that translates a product decision into buildable specifications. It sits between discovery and delivery in the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC), which is the end-to-end process of planning, building, testing, and releasing software. This post walks through a standard PRD structure and explains why each section exists.

1. Problem Statement

The document opens with the user problem, not the solution. A well-formed problem statement names the affected user, the friction, and the evidence. For example: "Finance administrators at mid-size firms spend an average of 4.2 hours per week manually reconciling invoice mismatches, based on 18 requirements gathering interviews conducted in Q2." Requirements gathering is the structured collection of user needs through interviews, surveys, and support ticket analysis. Anchoring the PRD in evidence prevents the most common failure mode: building a solution in search of a problem.

2. Goals and Success Metrics

This section states what the feature must achieve in measurable terms. A useful discipline is pairing each goal with a numeric target and a measurement source: "Reduce reconciliation time from 4.2 to under 1.5 hours per week, measured by in-product time tracking." If a goal cannot be measured, it should be rewritten until it can.

3. User Stories

A user story is a short requirement written from the user's perspective in the format: "As a [role], I want [capability], so that [benefit]." For the reconciliation example: "As a finance administrator, I want mismatched invoices flagged automatically, so that I only review exceptions." Stories keep the document oriented on outcomes rather than implementation details, which remain the engineering team's domain.

4. Acceptance Criteria

Acceptance criteria are the testable conditions that define when a story is complete. Each criterion must produce a binary pass or fail. For the flagging story: "Given an invoice whose total differs from the purchase order by more than 1%, the system marks it as an exception within 60 seconds of ingestion." Quality Assurance (QA), the discipline of verifying that software behaves as specified, builds its test cases directly from these criteria. Vague criteria produce vague tests, and vague tests ship defects.

5. Scope and Non-Goals

Listing what the feature will not do is as important as listing what it will. A non-goals section such as "This release does not support multi-currency reconciliation" prevents scope creep, the gradual expansion of requirements after work has begun. Scope creep is a schedule risk with compounding cost: an addition requested at 80% completion can force rework of finished components.

6. Dependencies, Risks, and Open Questions

The closing section records what the feature relies on (an upstream API, a data migration), what could fail, and what remains undecided. Leaving open questions visible is preferable to premature answers; it tells reviewers exactly where their input is needed.

Where the Document Lives

A PRD is a living document, not a one-time handoff. Teams typically maintain it in a collaborative workspace such as Confluence or Notion, linked directly to the corresponding development tickets so that engineers, designers, and QA reference a single source of truth. When a decision changes, the PRD changes first, and the change history becomes a record of why the product evolved the way it did.

Practical Summary

The structure above follows a deliberate logic: evidence establishes the problem, metrics define success, stories express the need, acceptance criteria make it testable, non-goals contain it, and open questions invite review. A PRD that holds this chain together can be handed to any engineer on the team and produce the same product. That reproducibility is the test of whether the document is done.
