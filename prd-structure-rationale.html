Structuring a Product Requirements Document: Section-by-Section Rationale

Frameworks 3 min read · Didar Islam

A Product Requirements Document (PRD) is the document that turns a product decision into something engineers can actually build. It sits between the "figuring out what to build" stage and the "actually building it" stage of the product process. Here's what goes in each section, and why.

To keep this concrete, one example runs through the whole post: a feature that automatically flags mismatched invoices for finance teams.

1. Problem Statement

The document starts with the user's problem, not your solution. A good problem statement names three things: who is affected, what's frustrating them, and proof that it's real.

Example: "Finance administrators at mid-size firms spend an average of 4.2 hours a week manually finding invoice mismatches, based on 18 user interviews conducted in the second quarter."

That last part, the interviews, matters. Writing down the evidence stops the most common mistake in this process: building a feature that solves nothing, because nobody checked if the problem was real first.

2. Goals and Success Metrics

This section says what the feature needs to accomplish, in numbers you can actually check later. Pair every goal with a number and a way to measure it.

Example: "Reduce reconciliation time from 4.2 hours to under 1.5 hours per week, tracked using in-product time logs."

If you cannot write a goal with a number attached, keep rewriting it until you can.

3. User Stories

A user story is a short requirement written from the user's point of view, in this format: "As a [role], I want [capability], so that [benefit]."

For this feature: "As a finance administrator, I want mismatched invoices flagged automatically, so that I only have to review the ones that are actually wrong."

Stories keep the document focused on what the user needs, not how engineers will build it. The "how" stays with the engineering team.

4. Acceptance Criteria

Acceptance criteria are the specific, checkable conditions that tell you a story is actually done. Each one needs to have a clear yes or no answer, nothing fuzzy.

Example: "If an invoice total is more than 1% different from its purchase order, the system marks it as an exception within 60 seconds of receiving it."

The QA (Quality Assurance) team, the people who test that software actually works as intended, build their tests directly from criteria like this. If the criteria are vague, the tests will be vague too, and vague tests let bugs slip through.

5. Scope and Non-Goals

Saying what a feature will NOT do matters just as much as saying what it will do. Example: "This release does not support invoices in multiple currencies."

Without this, you get scope creep, meaning the requirements quietly grow after work has already started. Scope creep gets more expensive the later it happens. A change requested when the feature is 80% built can force the team to redo work that was already finished.

6. Dependencies, Risks, and Open Questions

This last section lists what the feature depends on (like another team's data or an outside system), what could go wrong, and what's still undecided.

Leaving a question open and visible is better than guessing at an answer just to fill the section. It tells whoever reads the document exactly where you need their input.

Where the Document Lives

A PRD should stay alive and get updated, not get written once and forgotten. Most teams keep it in a shared workspace like Confluence or Notion, linked to the actual engineering tickets, so everyone (engineers, designers, QA) is looking at the same source of truth. When a decision changes, update the PRD first, and keep the change history so there's a record of why the product ended up the way it did.

Bottom Line

Each section builds on the last: evidence proves the problem is real, metrics define what success looks like, stories describe the need in the user's words, acceptance criteria make it testable, non-goals keep it contained, and open questions invite the right people to weigh in. A good test for whether a PRD is actually finished: could you hand it to any engineer on the team and have them build the same product? If yes, it's done.
