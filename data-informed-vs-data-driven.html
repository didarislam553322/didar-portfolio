Data-Informed vs. Data-Driven Decision Making in Product Development

Strategy 3 min read · Didar Islam

People use these two phrases like they mean the same thing, but they describe two different ways of making a decision. Data-driven means the number decides, full stop. Data-informed means the number is one input, weighed alongside things like customer feedback, company strategy, and plain judgment. This post explains the difference, shows where each one breaks down, and gives a simple rule for choosing between them.

When to Let the Number Decide

Letting the data decide works well when a decision is repeatable, well-measured, and cheap to get wrong. Things like button placement, email subject lines, or tweaking a ranking algorithm are good examples. Run the test, let the winner win, and do not second-guess it with opinion.

Behind decisions like these sits a data pipeline: raw user actions get stored in a data warehouse (a large cloud database, like Snowflake), then cleaned up into usable tables using tools like dbt, then queried using SQL (the standard language for asking questions of a database), and the results get reviewed in something like Excel. When that pipeline is reliable, small, low-risk decisions should run through it without much debate.

Where "Let the Data Decide" Breaks Down

This approach fails in three common ways.

  1. Data only shows the past. No amount of usage data could have told a team to build a brand new type of product, because that data only describes behavior inside the product you already have.

  2. The metric becomes the goal, side effects and all. This is sometimes called surrogation, meaning the measurement quietly replaces the actual goal it was supposed to represent. A team chasing "session length" will build things that keep people on the app longer, whether or not that's actually good for them.

  3. Small samples get mistaken for real evidence. Say a pricing test shows Option B converting at 5.5%, versus 5.0% for Option A, based on 800 users each. That is 44 conversions versus 40, a gap small enough to happen by pure chance. A proper statistical check would not treat that as a real difference. To reliably detect a gap that small, you typically need several thousand users per option, not hundreds.

When to Bring in Judgment

Data-informed decisions add two things a database cannot give you. The first is qualitative feedback, meaning things like support tickets, customer interviews, and reviews, read carefully rather than just counted. Twenty interviews with customers who left can explain a "why" that ten thousand rows of usage data never will. The second is strategy: a company moving into a new market might correctly build a feature that today's usage data ranks last, because that data reflects the customers they have now, not the ones they're trying to win.

The risk here is real too: "informed" can quietly turn into "ignored," where data gets reviewed and then overruled just because someone senior said so. The fix is to write it down. When a decision goes against the data, note the reasoning and what you expect to happen, then check later if you were right. Anyone whose gut calls keep missing loses the benefit of the doubt.

A Simple Rule

Look at two things: how easy the decision is to undo, and how good your data actually is. If a decision is easy to reverse and well-measured, let the data decide. If it's hard to undo, or the real evidence is more feedback and strategy than numbers, bring in judgment, and write down why.

The real question is never "should we use data." It's whether the data you have is good enough to make the call by itself. When it is, trust it. When it isn't, say so out loud, and track whether your judgment turns out to be right.
